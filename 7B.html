Chapter 7 Exercise B

Posted on July 2, 2016 by Done Right

4. Suppose $\mb F= \C$ and $T\in\ca L(V)$. Prove that $T$ is normal if and only if all pairs of eigenvectors corresponding to distinct eigenvalues of $T$ are orthogonal and \[V=E(\lambda_1,T)\oplus\cdots\oplus E(\lambda_m,T),\] where $\lambda_1$, $\cdots$, $\lambda_m$ denote the distinct eigenvalues of $T$.

Solution: Suppose $T$ is normal, by 7.22 we have all pairs of eigenvectors corresponding to distinct eigenvalues of $T$ are orthogonal. By 7.24 and 5.41, we have\[V=E(\lambda_1,T)\oplus\cdots\oplus E(\lambda_m,T),\] where $\lambda_1$, $\cdots$, $\lambda_m$ denote the distinct eigenvalues of $T$.

Suppose all pairs of eigenvectors corresponding to distinct eigenvalues of $T$ are orthogonal and \[V=E(\lambda_1,T)\oplus\cdots\oplus E(\lambda_m,T),\] where $\lambda_1$, $\cdots$, $\lambda_m$ denote the distinct eigenvalues of $T$, then $V$ has an orthonormal basis consisting of eigenvectors of $T$. In fact, this basis can be chosen as the union of orthonormal basis of $E(\lambda_i,T)$, $i=1,\cdots,m$. Again by 7.24, we have $T$ is normal.

5. Suppose $\mb F= \R$ and $T\in\ca L(V)$. Prove that $T$ is self-adjoint if and only if all pairs of eigenvectors corresponding to distinct eigenvalues of $T$ are orthogonal and \[V=E(\lambda_1,T)\oplus\cdots\oplus E(\lambda_m,T),\] where $\lambda_1$, $\cdots$, $\lambda_m$ denote the distinct eigenvalues of $T$.

Solution: Suppose $T$ is self-adjoint, $v$ and $u$ are eigenvectors of $T$ corresponding to eigenvalues $\lambda$ and $\xi$ respectively, where $\lambda\ne \xi$. Then $T$ is self-adjoint \[ \langle Tv,u\rangle=\langle v,Tu\rangle, \]hence \[ \lambda \langle v,u\rangle=\xi\langle v,u\rangle. \]This implies $\langle v,u\rangle=0$ since $\lambda\ne \xi$. By 7.29 and 5.41, we have\[V=E(\lambda_1,T)\oplus\cdots\oplus E(\lambda_m,T),\] where $\lambda_1$, $\cdots$, $\lambda_m$ denote the distinct eigenvalues of $T$.

Suppose all pairs of eigenvectors corresponding to distinct eigenvalues of $T$ are orthogonal and \[V=E(\lambda_1,T)\oplus\cdots\oplus E(\lambda_m,T),\] where $\lambda_1$, $\cdots$, $\lambda_m$ denote the distinct eigenvalues of $T$, then $V$ has an orthonormal basis consisting of eigenvectors of $T$. In fact, this basis can be chosen as the union of orthonormal basis of $E(\lambda_i,T)$, $i=1,\cdots,m$. Again by 7.29, we have $T$ is self-adjoint.

6. Prove that a normal operator on a complex inner product space is selfadjoint if and only if all its eigenvalues are real. [The exercise above strengthens the analogy (for normal operators) between self-adjoint operators and real numbers.]

Solution: See Linear Algebra Done Right Solution Manual Chapter 7 Problem 9.

7. Suppose $V$ is a complex inner product space and $T\in \ca L(V)$ is a normal operator such that $T^9=T^8$. Prove that $T$ is self-adjoint and $T^2=T$.

Solution: See Linear Algebra Done Right Solution Manual Chapter 7 Problem 10.

8. Give an example of an operator $T$ on a complex vector space such that $T^9=T^8$ but $T^2\ne T$.

Solution: Consider $T\in\ca L(\C^3)$. Let $e_1=(1,0,0)$, $e_2=(0,1,0)$ and $e_3=(0,0,1)$. Define $T$ such that\[Te_1=0,\quad Te_2=e_1,\quad Te_3=e_2.\]Then we have $T^3e_1=0$, $T^3e_2=T^2e_1=0$ and $T^3e_3=T^2e_2=Te_1=0$, i.e. $T^3=0$. It follows that $T^9=T^8$. On the other hand, we have $T^2e_2=Te_1=0$ and $Te_2=e_1$, it follows that $T^2\ne T$.

9. Suppose $V$ is a complex inner product space. Prove that every normal operator on $V$ has a square root. (An operator $S\in\ca L(V)$ is called a square root of $T\in\ca L(V)$ if $S^2=T$.)

Solution: See Linear Algebra Done Right Solution Manual Chapter 7 Problem 11.

10. Give an example of a real inner product space $V$ and $T\in\ca L(V)$ and real numbers $b,c$ with $b^2 < 4c$ such that $T^2+bT+cI$ is not invertible. [The exercise above shows that the hypothesis that $T$ is self-adjoint is
needed in 7.26, even for real vector spaces.]

Solution: See Linear Algebra Done Right Solution Manual Chapter 7 Problem 12.

11. Prove or give a counterexample: every self-adjoint operator on $V$ has a cube root. (An operator $S\in\ca L(V)$ is called a cube root of $T\in\ca L(V)$ if
$S^3=T$.)

Solution: See Linear Algebra Done Right Solution Manual Chapter 7 Problem 13.

12. Suppose $T\in\ca L(V)$ is self-adjoint, $\lambda\in \mb F$, and $\epsilon > 0$. Suppose there exists $v\in V$ such that $\|v\|=1$ and\[\|Tv-\lambda v\|<\epsilon.\]Prove that $T$ has an eigenvalue $\lambda'$ such that $|\lambda-\lambda'|<\epsilon$.

Solution: See Linear Algebra Done Right Solution Manual Chapter 7 Problem 14.

14. Suppose $U$ is a finite-dimensional real vector space and $T\in\ca L(U)$. Prove that $U$ has a basis consisting of eigenvectors of $T$ if and only if there is an inner product on $U$ that makes $T$ into a self-adjoint operator.

Solution: See Linear Algebra Done Right Solution Manual Chapter 7 Problem 15.

15. Find the matrix entry below that is covered up.

Solution: Let the entry be $x$. Then by definition we have\[\Bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}
1 & 1 & 0\\
0 & 1 & 1\\
1 & 0 & x
\end{smallmatrix}\Bigr)\Bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}
1 & 0 & 1\\
1 & 1 & 0\\
0 & 1 & x
\end{smallmatrix}\Bigr)=\Bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}
1 & 0 & 1\\
1 & 1 & 0\\
0 & 1 & x
\end{smallmatrix}\Bigr)\Bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}
1 & 1 & 0\\
0 & 1 & 1\\
1 & 0 & x
\end{smallmatrix}\Bigr).\]Consider the (1,3) entry of the two products, we get $x=1$.
